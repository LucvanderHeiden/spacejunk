TO INFINITY AND BEYOND

In the second half of the 20th century, humanity ventured beyond Earth into the unknown. The space race between the United States and the Soviet Union unfolded, culminating in the historic launch of Sputnik 1 in 1957 and Yuri Gagarin's orbital feat in 1961. The United States followed with their Apollo missions and in landed, for the first time in history, on the moon in 1969.

Advancements in satellite technology defined the 1980s, impacting communication, weather monitoring, and scientific research. Satellite communications expanded to carry television programs, transforming how people receive information. Satellites played a pivotal role in discovering the ozone hole over Antarctica, pinpointing forest fires, and providing images of the Chernobyl nuclear disaster in 1986. Astronomical satellites, such as the Hubble Space Telescope and the more recent JWST, contributed to the exploration of new stars and offered a fresh perspective on the center of our galaxy. The knowlegde we gained by going into space changed the way we look at the universe and our place within it.

The International Space Station, a symbol of international collaboration, has served as a continuous orbiting laboratory since the year 2000. This high-flying laboratory pushes humanity towards global cooperation in space exploration as it has been continuously occupied and serviced by a variety of visiting spacecraft, fostering collaboration among astronauts, cosmonauts, and space tourists from 17 different nations.

TRAFFIC PARADISE

These days it is difficult to imagine our lives without the constant and instant connectivity that we're used to. We don't just use our mobile phones to stay connected to our friends and family, we use them for vital information during our jobs, travelling and emergencies. However, space is getting busier every day. Where there were once only a handful of satellites, Earth is now surrounded by more than 1 million pieces of debris, which is travelling at enormously high speeds. If we don't do anything to stop this, space will get more and more crowded until one day we've reached a tipping point. Debris will collide with other debris, in turn creating a lot more debris, this will once again collide with more debris, creating a never ending chain reaction, until our entire planet is practically blanketed with debris. This hypothetical phenomenon is the called the Kessler problem and if we don't change our ways, it could be our future.

WHAT KIND OF SPACE DEBRIS IS OUT THERE?

Space debris ranges from broken down satellites the size of a bus, to small pieces of metal no larger than a pebble. But where does it all come from? And how does it end up in space?

01

SATELLITES

There are currently more than 8,000 satellites orbiting Earth. Most of these satellites are in lower earth orbit (LEO). This means they travel in orbit around 500-1,000 kilometer above the Earth. The five most common uses for these satellites are: communication, Earth observation, technology development, navigation and space science. The amount of satellites being used for communication is more than all of the other types of satellites combined.

Did you know that the average satellite used for communication or navigation is about the same size and weight as a bus?

02

CONSTRUCTION PARTS

Currently there are more than 1 million pieces of debris in orbit around the Earth. Most of these are only a couple centimeters in size. This may not sound like much of an issue, but these parts are travelling at a speed of 7 kilometers per second. At that speed it would take you only about 90 minutes to travel around the Earth completely. Because of this these pieces of debris practically act like a bullet, only heavier, larger and faster.

These are parts that have potentially broken off of satellites or in some cases they are the remains of crashed satellites. In 2009 two communications satellites collided in space. This collision caused at least 1,000 pieces of debris larger than 10 centimers. Because of the high velocity during the impact this debris was scattered in a wide area.

03

ROCKETS

When a rocket launches into space it will do so in stages. The boosters furthest on the bottom will expend all their full and the rocket will then decouple them, so as to lose unnecessary weight. Usually these parts will then fall back into the atmosphere where they either burn up or land somewhere on Earth to be recovered. However, sometimes these parts do not fall back to Earth, but instead keep floating around in space. These parts can be very large and therefor quite dangerous.

04

MISCELLANEOUS DEBRIS

There's also all kinds of random stuff floating around in space. For example in 1965 astronaut Ed White accidentally misplaced a spare glove, which is now still floating somewhere in orbit around the Earth. Astronaut Piers Sellers managed to lose a spatula in space in 2005. Lastly, during a 2007 spacewalk astronaut Sunita Williams lost grip of a special space camera, which proceeded to drift away from the ship.

01

IMPACT ON DAILY LIFE

The looming threat of the Kessler Syndrome poses a direct risk to the daily lives of ordinary individuals. As the density of space debris continues to escalate, the potential consequences for our daily routines become increasingly tangible. Essential services that rely on satellites, such as weather forecasts, global navigation systems, and communication networks, may face disruptions. This means that the accuracy of weather predictions we rely on, the seamless functioning of our GPS navigation, and even the reliability of our everyday communication tools could be compromised. The implications are clear that the average person may find their daily life significantly influenced by the growing menace of space debris, highlighting the urgency of addressing this issue to safeguard our technological dependencies.

02

IMPACT ON SCIENCE AND EXPLORATION

Imagine our cosmic playground turning into a chaotic space disco ball, thanks to the Kessler Syndrome. The mess up there isn't just a bummer for your stargazing plans; it's giving scientists and space explorers a real headache. Telescopes that once effortlessly captured the wonders of the universe might start playing hide-and-seek with rogue debris, making it hard to get those jaw-dropping shots of distant galaxies. Think about our space probes and rovers trying to navigate through a cosmic obstacle course. It's like trying to skateboard in a crowded mall during a shopping frenzy. Plus, launching new missions becomes riskier than playing intergalactic roulette. The Kessler Syndrome isn't just cramping our style; it's throwing a celestial spanner into the gears of our scientific exploration. So, if you were hoping for a front-row seat to humanity's cosmic adventures, you might want to root for a debris-free sky.

WHAT CAN BE DONE?

So what can be done about this problem? There are solutions that can either prevent more space debris in the future and solutions that can clean up the debris currently in orbit around our planet.

01

FORCED RE-ENTRY

This technique has already been succesfully tested before. The idea is to slow down a piece of debris, for example an old satellite, so that it starts falling out of orbit. At some point it will re-enter the Earth's atmosphere where it will face more and more atmospheric resistance. This in turn will cause the satellite to heat up too incredibly high temperatures, which will cause it to burn up completely. This is not only an effective way to get rid of large pieces of space debris, it also has the added benefit of causing a beautiful and unique sight; a falling star. Some innovators, like Daan Roosegaarde, are already looking into the potential to, with enough coordination, turn these events into falling star shows. These could perhaps replace the need for fireworks shows, which in turn create a lot of debris on our own planet.

02

RECYCLING IN SPACE

Recycling has become quite commonplace on our planet by now. However, this same push for recycling has not found its way into space yet. Repurposing and recycling space waste can offer a lot of advantages. By establishing a facility in Earth's orbit dedicated to repairing and repurposing satellites and discarded space objects, we can transform space trash into valuable resources for constructing future spacecraft and exploration outposts. This approach not only minimizes the need for new launches but also contributes to reducing overall space debris. Beyond the environmental benefits, recycling satellites can serve as a sustainable source of raw materials and potential revenue for funding future space endeavors. Additionally, this recycled technology could contribute to the development of advanced space-based instruments, such as cameras deployed on Gateway Earth to monitor the skies for potential asteroid threats.

03

MITIGATION THROUGH LEGISLATION

Maybe the most important thing is to have a better plan for the future of space exploitation. Using space has huge economic and strategic value for a lot of companies and even countries. Given the freedom to do what they want, these parties will continue to use space for as long as it's viable to them. Therefor, it is extremely important that international commitees, such as the UN, set in place guidelines and laws that counteract the overutilization of space. Some institutions like the NASA and ESA are already planning on becoming space-debris neutral in the near future. This will be of great help, yet if other companies or institutions do not follow the problem will merely be slowed down, not solved.
